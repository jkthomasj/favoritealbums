Favorite Albums

In no particular order

Electric Wizard

Dopethrone

Stoner Doom, 2000

The crown jewel of the Engish doom outfit, Dopethrone stands as a testament to heaviness across time and space. It's all about them riffs here - thunderingly heavy and drenched in reverb. There's so much groovy goodness and a few brilliant bluesy solos that just go so well along with the monsterously crushing sound. The Wizard is the brightest and best among Black Sabbath's bastard children and still haven't been dethroned almost two decades later.

Best Tracks

Dopethrone Funeralopolis

Massive Attack

Mezzanine

Trip Hop, 1998

To say that Mezzanine is an extraordinary album is an understatement. From the brooding darkness of Angel to the eerily exotic Inertia Creeps, the record has spawned a number of tracks that have earwormed their way into public consciousness. Banksy, I mean, Robert Del Naja, and crew have poured their highly mercurial souls into an album for the ages.

Best Tracks

Angel Teardrop

Morbid Saint

Spectrum of Death

Thrash Metal, 1990

When it comes to thrash, The Big Four (Slayer, Metallica, Megadeth, and Anthrax) always seem to get the most praise in more mainstream metal circles. But Morbid Saint, who out Thrashes and destroys all four of them in just one album, sadly remains underground. The Saint puts out some of the meanest, fastest and mind-meltingly furious music ever in Spectrum of Death. Just listen to that opening riff from Assassin and tell me if there's anything like it. The best Thrash Metal album ever made. Period. Sorry Demolition Hammer, Kreator, Sadus, Dark Angel and the rest of all of those magnificent Thrash titans out there but Spectrum of Death is just on a whole different level. I'm also not entirely sure if Pat Lind is actually human and not a demon from the depths - I mean, he certainly does make an admission in Beyond the Gates of Hell - "Beyond the gates of hell. Lurking in the dark. Forever I will be, beyond the gates of hell"

Best Tracks

Lock Up Your Children Assassin Crying for Death

Kyuss

Welcome to Sky Valley

Desert Rock, 1993

Kyuss or Jo$h Homme's earlier band before he went on to find Queen$ of the $tone Age, gifted us the insanely addictive Welcome to Sky Valley back in the mid 90s and gave us a taste of the dry california deserts. The range of sounds on offer here are a billion times superior to anything QOTSA ever made. I don't know what led to Kyuss' demise but it's indeed a tragedy. The guitar tone on this album is indescribable - that opening riff of Gardenia - pure molten magma. I don't think we'll ever get an album like this ever again from anyone.

Best Tracks

Gardenia Demon Cleaner

Tool

Lateralus

Progressive Rock, 2001

It's Tool and it's their greatest album. There's nothing more to add. Go ask a Tool fan what they think about Lateralus and you'll probably hear an hour long lecture depending on their passion for the band - and most Tool fans are passionate about the band. Although 10000 Days is a more dazzling record, Lateralus still remains untouched. Their latest outing after 15 years is decent for sure and Grammy win aside, ask yourself, how does it compare to Lateralus? That's a rhetorical question btw. And, just to refresh your memories, Schism won a Grammy too. Also, Maynard, please stop with the winemaking and get back to music!

Best Tracks

Lateralus The Grudge

Aphex Twin

Selected Ambient Works 85-92

Ambient Electronica, 2001

Beautiful primitivism - that's the essence of Aphex Twin's debut. The songs are minimal and simplistic but contain so much of that primal brilliance that made Richard D James the IDM icon he is today. Totally essential for anyone interested in real Electronic music and not the template-based 4x4 club music that seems ubiquitous these days.

Best Tracks

Xtal Heliosphan

Agalloch

The Mantle

Folk Metal, 2002

It's a shame that these guys aren't around anymore. Personally, it was a tough choice having to choose this over their debut Pale Folklore, but what finally tipped the scale was the polish and ingenuity applied to the ideas established in its predecessor. The Mantle features an indescribably beautiful marriage of melancholy and hope that leaves you mystified about your own feelings, and for some reason, longing for some time alone in nature.

Best Tracks

In the Shadow of Our Pale Companion The Hawthorne Passage

God Is An Astronaut

All Is Violent, All Is Bright

Post Rock, 2005

GIAA crafts a sound that can only be described as breathtaking in this Post Rock masterpiece. What I like the most about them is how they don't go all saccharine like their close cousins *cough* Explosions in the Sky *cough* which is something most Post Rockers tend to do. There is just the right mix of emotional buildups with heavy crescendoes here that contributes to creating an absolute classic.

Best Tracks

All Is Violent, All Is Bright Suicide By Star

Venetian Snares

Rossz Csillag Alatt Született

Classical Breakcore, 2005

The most outlandish entry on this list is a frenzied mix of classical strings and pummelling breakbeats. The mad Canuck Aaron Funk is on another dimension here as he layers grandiose classical strings with hyperkinetic beats that keeps listeners on ther toes. The highlight of the album is the majestic Hajnal which starts off with what can only be explained as "anxious" strings that keep building up before devolving into a spaced out jazzy jam that suddenly returns to the strings before the drums kick and the rest is just an unbelievable mix of chaos and grandeur that has to simply be heard to be believed. I read somewhere that Funk learned the trumpet and electric violin to produce this album, I mean, who would actually do something like that?

Best Tracks

Hajnal Szamár Madár

At the Gates

Slaughter of the Soul

Melodic Death Metal, 1995

They say imitation is the highest form of flattery. But in this case, it's rather tragic and led to a significant degree of creative death. No, I'm not talking about this album but the sheer number of bastard children it spawned in the form of all that unimaginable amounts of "core" that dominated the 2000s. I'm not really sure of the scene currently but at that time, it was just a hundred million bands all ripping off those cursed catchy riffs in this album. I mean surely, SotS is a masterpiece but it has been raped and abused so much. When a singular album gives birth to a whole genre, you can call it significant.

Best Tracks

Slaughter of the Soul Suicide Nation

A Perfect Circle

Thirteenth Step

Alternative Rock, 2003

Maynard James Keenan's more accessible and less intense outlet APC's Thirteenth Step is a gorgeous album that works so well in its entirety. There are a couple of tracks here that have eternal replay value. An instant rock classic that will live on through the ages. What I love most about the record is that it communicates an extremely diverse range of emotions with ease.

Best Tracks

The Package The Noose Weak and Powerless

Squarepusher

Ultravisitor

Drum n Bass, 2004

"Ultravisitor is my spectacle of beauty and of terror. It is unknowable, and will never be understood by anybody, least of all its creator. - Tom Jenkins (aka Squarepusher). A perfect definition for an album that has such a whole universe inside it and still manages to be tight and cohesive. My absolute favorite thing about the album is how it manages to convey this feeling of hope in between all the pops and the glitches and what not. Also, you can just listen to Iambic 9 Poetry on loop all day and not get tired of it.

Best Tracks

Ultravisitor Iambic 9 Poetry

Boris

Pink

Stoner Metal, Noise 2005

This relentless Japanese trio released Pink to almost universal acclaim back in 2005 and it's an insanely addictive album for sure. I've almost no idea of what they're singing about but the energy and catchiness behind tracks like Pink, Woman on the Screen, Nothing Special have me smashing the repeat button endlessly. Just watch out for that final track Just Abandoned Myself. That's all I gotta say.

Best Tracks

Nothing Special Afterburner

Mudvayne

L.D. 50

Nu Metal, 2000

Okay, I'll admit it, I went through that phase back in High School. But hey, this isn't a guilty pleasure, in fact, it's a masterpiece that features timesless classics that their other more commercial brethren weren't able to touch. I don't think any of their contemporaries ever displayed the depth that Mudvayne did here. It's such a shame they are no more.
